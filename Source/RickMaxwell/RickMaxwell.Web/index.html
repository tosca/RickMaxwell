Scrapbox Statement

During this year -like no other-I turned to my studio as a place of solace. The sculpture I created in it became my coping mechanism in a year gone mad. This virus has created many boundaries and in my situation one of my boundaries was the elimination of easily acquiring materials -especially the bendable wood I receive from the west coast. At first I saw this as an inconvenience, but what began as a hindrance soon became an asset, because it limited my decision making to selecting wood only found in my scrap boxes. I was limited to the shapes and colors on hand and began constructing compositions not through preconceived form but according to the relationships of shape, color and texture of the material at hand.This resulted in a series of sculptures that are smaller than my comfort zone normally allows but unified, none-the-less, through principles found in all of my work.
I'd like to hear from you.

RICK MAXWELL \ bio \ contact

In the past 40 years, I've written many 'Artist Statements'. Some were insightful, some were intellectual, some were humorous and a few were even embarrassing. Now, after all this time, what it's come down to is exactly where it started. I enjoy being an artist because I enjoy the simple task of making the art. For me the process has always had a stronger allure than standing in front of the finished piece. I love the tools I work with, the materials I work in, and the empathy I experience as I move through the process. Simply said, "I'm an artist because I'm addicted to making art."

Rick Maxwell
